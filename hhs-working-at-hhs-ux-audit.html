Top critical failures

1
No employer value proposition
The hub page opens with a generic statement and quickly moves into benefits policy. There is no mission-driven “why HHS” narrative, no proof points, and no differentiation from other federal employers.
2
Job search depends too heavily on client-side rendering
The search experience is not broken, but it is fragile: meaningful results arrive only after JavaScript runs. Users need a visible loading state, failure state, and plain fallback link to HHS-filtered USAJOBS results so the path still works when the widget is delayed or unavailable.
3
Freshness and evidence signals are uneven
Some pages show active maintenance, especially the disabilities hiring content. Other content still needs stronger freshness cues, current compensation links, transcript availability, and a visible governance cadence.

Detailed findings by heuristic

H1 · 4/10 Visibility of system status
The job search needs explicit status feedback: loading copy while the JavaScript widget initializes, a “no results” state, a widget-failure state, and a persistent fallback link. Without those states, users can mistake delayed loading for an empty page.
H2 · 3/10 Match between system and the real world
Pay, retirement, and benefits copy leads with federal HR terminology such as GS classification, locality pay surveys, and FERS/CSRS. Candidates need plain-language answers: what can I earn, what benefits matter, and how do I compare options?
H3 · 5/10 User control and freedom
The sidebar gives basic within-section movement. However, the long benefits page lacks anchor navigation, external links can interrupt context, and the job search does not offer enough recovery routes if the JavaScript widget fails.
H4 · 5/10 Consistency and standards
The section generally follows the broader HHS.gov template, but subpages vary in depth and structure. The disabilities page is actionable and structured; the hub reads like a benefits policy dump.
H5 · 4/10 Error prevention
A JavaScript-dependent search flow should prevent perceived failure by shipping fallback content and clear errors. Link governance should also avoid stale salary or benefits references.
H6 · 2/10 Recognition rather than recall
The site offers no browsable role families, operating divisions, student/recent graduate path, or veteran path. Users must already know what to search for and how federal hiring works.
H7 · 5/10 Flexibility and efficiency of use
The Schedule A page shows what efficient shortcuts can look like, with direct links and contact information. General applicants need comparable shortcuts: recent postings, remote roles, entry-level openings, and division filters.
H8 · 3/10 Aesthetic and minimalist design
The hub page presents too many benefits details in a dense wall of text. Cards, summaries, and progressive disclosure would make the same content easier to scan.
H9 · 6/10 Help users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors
The disabilities page gives alternate pathways through USAJOBS, SPPC directories, and HR contacts. The general hub and JavaScript job search should provide equivalent recovery guidance.
H10 · 5/10 Help and documentation
The section relies on OPM for much of the explanation. First-time federal applicants need an HHS-specific onboarding path, hiring-process FAQ, and links that preserve context.

Navigation depth issues

IA
Hub underuses the CTA path
The search CTA appears early, but there are few secondary CTAs after benefit sections to re-engage users who scroll through long content.
JS
Search visibility depends on runtime state
Because results load via JavaScript, the static IA does not expose job categories, role families, or a durable search fallback.
Vid
I Am HHS is a flat video list
Stories are not grouped by division, career type, role level, location, or pathway. Discoverability depends on scrolling.

P1 — Immediate fixes

Harden the JavaScript job search experience
Keep the working search widget, but add a visible loading state, empty state, error state, and a prominent fallback link to HHS-filtered USAJOBS results. Add a short explanation that results are loading from USAJOBS or the search provider.
Critical1–2 days effort
Add transcript and context links to all I Am HHS videos
Provide captions, transcript links, text summaries, and role metadata for each video. This improves accessibility, search visibility, and candidate self-identification.
AccessibilityMedium effort
Audit compensation and benefits links
Replace stale year-specific compensation links with current or durable landing pages where appropriate, and document ownership for quarterly link checks.
Trust30-minute fix + process

P2 — Near-term improvements

Redesign the hub page with scannable benefit cards
Replace the dense benefits wall with a card grid, short summaries, and progressive disclosure for detailed policy content. Repeat job-search CTAs after major sections.
High impact2–3 week design + dev
Add an employer value proposition above the fold
Lead with a mission-driven headline, proof points about HHS scale and public impact, and employee story excerpts that connect to current openings.
Conversion lift1 week content + design
Create Students & Recent Grads and Veterans pathway pages
Surface Pathways, PMF, internships, Veterans’ Preference, VEOA, VRA, Schedule A interplay, and practical USAJOBS search tips for each audience.
New audiences3–4 week effort

P3 — Strategic redesign

Build audience-segmented career pathways
Add a “Who are you?” entry pattern for mid-career professionals, students/recent grads, veterans, people with disabilities, and current federal employees.
High impact8–12 week project
Integrate division-level career pages
Connect candidates to CDC, NIH, FDA, HRSA, CMS, and other division-specific missions, skills, locations, and active openings.
Cross-divisional6-month initiative
Establish a content governance cadence
Assign owners, quarterly freshness checks, link validation, transcript checks, and job-search widget monitoring. The disabilities page demonstrates that active upkeep is achievable.
